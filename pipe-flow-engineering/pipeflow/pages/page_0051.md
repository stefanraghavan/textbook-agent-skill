Substituting these into Equation 4.15, we obtain:

$$
Y=\sqrt{\frac{K\dot{w}^{2}}{144\Delta p\,2gA^{2}}\frac{A\bar{V}}{\dot{w}}}
=\sqrt{\frac{K\dot{w}V_{1}}{144\Delta p\,2g}\frac{4(144)}{\pi d^{2}}}
$$

$$
=\sqrt{\frac{4K\dot{w}V_{1}}{2\pi g\Delta p\,d^{2}}}
=\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi g}}\sqrt{\frac{K\dot{w}V_{1}}{\Delta p\,d^{2}}}
$$

$$
=0.14067\sqrt{\frac{K\dot{w}V_{1}}{\Delta p\,d^{2}}}.
$$

Charts of expansion factor $Y$ versus $\Delta p/p_{1}$ (or $\Delta P/P_{1}$, because $\Delta p/p_{1}=\Delta P/P_{1}$) prepared this way are given in Figure 4.3, for $\gamma=1.4$, and in Figure 4.4, for $\gamma=1.3$.

Diatomic gases, such as nitrogen, oxygen, and hydrogen, typically have a ratio of specific heats ($\gamma$) close to 1.4, and polyatomic gases, such as carbon dioxide, methane, and steam, have a ratio of specific heats of about 1.3. If $\gamma$ is not constant, you may not want to use these charts. You may need to determine the average value of $\gamma$ to determine if it is close to the chart value. To utilize the charts, known values of $\Delta p$ and $p_{1}$ may be used to find $\Delta p/p_{1}$; from this $Y$ may be read from the chart, and, using Equation 4.13, the flow rate $\dot{w}$ may be found. If $\dot{w}$ is known but $\Delta p$ is not, then it must be estimated, and the resulting $\dot{w}$ must be compared with the known value. From these, a correction in $\Delta p$ may be found and the process repeated until the flow rates match.
