The overall diameter ratio $\beta$ equals $d_T/d$, and the exit step diameter ratio $\beta_E$ equals $d_T/d_E$ for the stepped diffuser. For diffuser included angle $\alpha_D$ less than $20^\circ$, the loss coefficient of a stepped conical diffuser can be approximately determined by the following equation adapted from Equation 10.6 for a rounded contraction and from Equation 11.11 for a stepped diffuser:

$$
K_T \approx 0.0696\left(1-0.569\frac{r}{d_T}\right)\left(1-\sqrt{\frac{r}{d_T}}\beta\right)(1-\beta^5)\lambda^2
+(\lambda-1)^2+f_T\frac{l_T}{d_T}+8.30(\tan(\alpha_D/2))^{1.75}(1-\beta_E^2)^2
+\frac{f_D(1-\beta_E^4)}{8\sin(\alpha_D/2)}+(\beta_E^2-\beta^2)^2
$$

$$
(\alpha \leq 20^\circ)\qquad (0 \geq \beta_E \leq 1),
$$

$$
(14.3)
$$

where the jet contraction coefficient $\lambda$ is given by:

$$
\lambda=1+0.622\left(1-0.30\sqrt{\frac{r}{d_T}}-0.70\frac{r}{d_T}\right)^4
(1-0.215\beta^2-0.785\beta^5).
$$

$$
(13.7,\ \text{repeated})
$$

The rounding contour may be the arc of a circle, or it may take the form of an ellipse, lemniscate, or other smoothly curved shape. For a circular inlet, the rounding radius $r$ is simply the radius of the circle. In the case of an elliptical inlet contour, the rounding radius can be expressed as:

$$
r=\sqrt[3]{r_1^2r_2},
$$

$$
(10.5,\ \text{repeated})
$$

where $r_1$ and $r_2$ are the semimajor (longitudinal) and semiminor (radial) axes, respectively.

When the nozzle/Venturi is not stepped, $\beta_E$ equals $\beta$ in Equations 14.3 and the last term, the sudden expansion term, vanishes.
