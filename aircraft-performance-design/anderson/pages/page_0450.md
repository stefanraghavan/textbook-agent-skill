PART 3 • Airplane Design

We will make use of the tail volume ratios, defined as follows:

Horizontal tail

$$
V_{\mathrm{HT}}=\frac{l_{\mathrm{HT}}S_{\mathrm{HT}}}{\bar{c}S}
$$

[8.60]

Vertical tail

$$
V_{\mathrm{VT}}=\frac{l_{\mathrm{VT}}S_{\mathrm{VT}}}{bS}
$$

[8.61]

where $V_{\mathrm{HT}}$ and $V_{\mathrm{VT}}$ are the horizontal and vertical tail volume ratios, respectively, $l_{\mathrm{HT}}$ is the horizontal distance between the c.g. of the airplane and the aerodynamic center of the horizontal tail, $l_{\mathrm{VT}}$ is the horizontal distance between the c.g. of the airplane and the aerodynamic center of the vertical tail, $S_{\mathrm{HT}}$ is the planform area of the horizontal tail, $S_{\mathrm{VT}}$ is the sideview area of the vertical tail, $\bar{c}$ is the mean aerodynamic chord of the wing, $b$ is the wingspan, and $S$ is the wing planform area. Based on previous single-engine general aviation airplanes, suggested values of these volume ratios (from Raymer, Ref. 25) are

$$
V_{\mathrm{HT}}=0.7
$$

[8.62]

$$
V_{\mathrm{VT}}=0.04
$$

[8.63]

We will use these values for our design.

The conventional location for the horizontal tail is centered on the tail end of the fuselage, as shown in Fig. 8.20a. There are many other possible tail configurations, such as the T tail (the horizontal tail mounted at the top of the vertical tail) shown in Fig. 8.20b, and the cruciform tail shown in Fig. 8.20c. The configuration shown in Fig. 8.20a is called *conventional* simply because it is found on over 70% of airplanes. It is favored because of its low structural weight compared to the other configurations in Fig. 8.20 while at the same time providing reasonable stability and control. However, the horizontal tail should be sufficiently far back that at stall the wake of the horizontal tail does not mask the rudder on the vertical tail.
