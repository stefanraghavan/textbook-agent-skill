The approach angle is usually small for most cases. For example, Raymer (Ref. 25) states that for transport aircraft $\theta_a \leq 3^\circ$. Hence, $\cos \theta_a \approx 1$ and from Eq. (6.101), $L \approx W$. In this case, Eq. (6.103) can be written as

$$
\sin \theta_a = \frac{1}{L/D} - \frac{T}{W}
$$

**[6.104]**

The flare height $h_f$, shown in Fig. 6.17, can be calculated from the construction shown in Fig. 6.19 as follows.

$$
h_f = R - R \cos \theta_f
$$

**[6.105]**

However, because the circular arc flight path of the flare is tangent to both the approach path and the ground, as shown in Fig. 6.19, $\theta_f = \theta_a$. Hence, Eq. (6.105) becomes

$$
h_f = R(1 - \cos \theta_a)
$$

**[6.106]**

In Eq. (6.106), $R$ is obtained from Eq. (6.41) by assuming that $V_\infty$ varies from $V_a = 1.3V_{\text{stall}}$ for commercial aircraft and $1.2V_{\text{stall}}$ for military aircraft to $V_{TD} = 1.15V_{\text{stall}}$ for commercial aircraft and $1.1V_{\text{stall}}$ for military aircraft, yielding an average velocity during the flare of $V_f = 1.23V_{\text{stall}}$ for commercial airplanes and $1.15V_{\text{stall}}$ for military airplanes. With the load factor $n$ stipulated as $n = 1.2$, Eq. (6.41) yields

$$
R = \frac{V_f^2}{0.2g}
$$

**[6.107]**

Finally, with $R$ given by Eq. (6.107) and $\theta_a$ from Eq. (6.104), $h_f$ can be calculated from Eq. (6.106). In turn, $s_a$ is obtained from Fig. 6.17 as

$$
s_a = \frac{50 - h_f}{\operatorname{Tan}\theta_a}
$$
