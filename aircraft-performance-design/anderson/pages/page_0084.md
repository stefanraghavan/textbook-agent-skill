Does the aerodynamic center, as defined above, exist? For the time being, we assume its existence and denote its location on the airfoil by the fixed point labeled a.c. in Fig. 2.14. This point is located a distance $x_{a.c.}$ from the quarter-chord. Taking moments about the point a.c., we have

$$
M_{a.c.} = Lx_{a.c.} + M_{c/4}
$$

[2.10]

Dividing Eq. (2.10) by $q_\infty Sc$, we have

$$
\frac{M_{a.c.}}{q_\infty Sc} = \frac{L}{q_\infty S}\left(\frac{x_{a.c.}}{c}\right) + \frac{M_{c/4}}{q_\infty Sc}
$$

or

$$
c_{m_{a.c.}} = c_l\left(\frac{x_{a.c.}}{c}\right) + c_{m_{c/4}}
$$

[2.11]

Differentiating Eq. (2.11) with respect to angle of attack $\alpha$ gives

$$
\frac{dc_{m_{a.c.}}}{d\alpha} = \frac{dc_l}{d\alpha}\left(\frac{x_{a.c.}}{c}\right) + \frac{dc_{m_{c/4}}}{d\alpha}
$$

[2.12]

Note that in Eq. (2.12) we are treating $x_{a.c.}$ as a *fixed* point on the airfoil, defined as that point about which moments are independent of the angle of attack. If such a fixed point does exist, it should be consistent with Eq. (2.12) where the derivative $dc_{m_{a.c.}}/d\alpha$ is set equal to zero (since $c_{m_{a.c.}}$ is constant with $\alpha$, by definition of the aerodynamic center). In this case, Eq. (2.12) becomes

$$
0 = \frac{dc_l}{d\alpha}\left(\frac{x_{a.c.}}{c}\right) + \frac{dc_{m_{c/4}}}{d\alpha}
$$
