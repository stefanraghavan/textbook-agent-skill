The experimental value for the lift slope for the NACA 2412 airfoil is easily measured from the data given in Fig. 2.6a; this author measures a value of $a_0 = 0.105$ (try it yourself). From the generic lift curve shown in Fig. 2.7, note that there is a finite value of $c_l$ at zero angle of attack, and that the airfoil must be pitched down to some negative angle of attack for the lift to be zero. This angle of attack is denoted by $\alpha_{L=0}$ in Fig. 2.7; for the NACA 2412 airfoil, the data in Fig. 2.6a show that $\alpha_{L=0} = -2.2^\circ$. All positively cambered airfoils have negative zero-lift angles of attack. In contrast, a symmetric airfoil has $\alpha_{L=0} = 0^\circ$, and a negatively cambered airfoil (such as the NACA 2412 airfoil turned upside down) has a positive $\alpha_{L=0}$. (Negatively cambered airfoils are of little practical interest in aeronautics.)
