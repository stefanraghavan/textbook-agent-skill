Since

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}=\mathbf{V}
$$

then

$$
\text{Power}=\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{V}
\qquad [5.51]
$$

Equation (5.51) is the more general version of Eq. (3.2), which holds when the force and velocity are in the same direction.

Let us now apply Eq. (5.51) to an airplane in straight and level flight, as sketched in Fig. 5.2. The velocity of the airplane is $V_\infty$. In Section 5.3, the concept of thrust required $T_R$ was introduced, where $T_R=D$. In this section, we introduce the analogous concept of *power required*, denoted by $P_R$. Since in Fig. 5.2 both $T$ and $V_\infty$ are horizontal, the dot product in Eq. (5.51) gives for the power required

$$
P_R=T_RV_\infty
\qquad [5.52]
$$

For some aspects of airplane performance, power rather than thrust is more germane, as we will soon see.

## 5.6.1 Graphical Approach

A graphical plot of $P_R$ versus $V_\infty$ for a given airplane at a given altitude is called the *power required curve*. The power required curve is easily obtained by multiplying thrust required by velocity via Eq. (5.52).
