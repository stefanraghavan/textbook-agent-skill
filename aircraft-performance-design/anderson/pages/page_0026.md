However, this was to be the zenith of Langley's success. Spurred by the exigency of the Spanish-American War, Langley was given a $50,000 grant from the War Department to construct and fly a full-scale, person-carrying aerodrome. He hired an assistant, Charles Manly, who was a fresh, young graduate of the Sibley School of Mechanical Engineering at Cornell University. Together, they set out to build the required flying machine. The advent of the gasoline-powered internal-combustion engine in Europe convinced them that the aerodrome should be powered by a gasoline-fueled reciprocating engine turning a propeller. Langley had calculated that, for his new aerodrome, he needed an engine that would produce at least 12 hp and weigh no more than 120 lb. This horsepower-to-weight ratio was well above that available in any engine of the time; indeed, Balzer Company in New York, under subcontract from Langley to design and build such an engine, went bankrupt trying. Manly then personally took over the engine design in the basement of the original “castle” building of the Smithsonian, and by 1901 he had assembled a radically designed five-cylinder radial engine, shown in Fig. 1.9. This engine weighed only 124 lb and produced a phenomenal 52.4 hp. It was to be the best airplane power plant designed until the beginning of World War I.
