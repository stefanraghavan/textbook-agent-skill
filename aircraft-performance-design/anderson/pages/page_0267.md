At a given altitude, the velocity at which an airplane stalls is determined by both $(C_L)_{\max}$ and the wing loading. For steady, level flight,

$$
L = W = \frac{1}{2}\rho_\infty V_\infty^2 S C_L
$$

Hence,

$$
V_\infty = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\rho_\infty}\frac{W}{S}\frac{1}{C_L}}
$$

[5.66]

When $(C_L)_{\max}$ is inserted into Eq. (5.66), the corresponding value of $V_\infty$ is the stalling velocity $V_{\text{stall}}$:

$$
V_{\text{stall}} = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\rho_\infty}\frac{W}{S}\frac{1}{(C_L)_{\max}}}
$$

[5.67]

From Eq. (5.67), clearly $V_{\text{stall}}$ depends on altitude (via $\rho_\infty$), wing loading $W/S$, and maximum lift coefficient $(C_L)_{\max}$. In particular, examining Eq. (5.67), we see that

1. Velocity $V_{\text{stall}}$ increases with increasing altitude (decreasing $\rho_\infty$). Reconnaissance airplanes that fly at extremely high altitudes, such as the Lockheed U-2, fly at velocities near stall because $C_L$ must be large in order to generate enough lift to equal the weight in the rarefied air.
