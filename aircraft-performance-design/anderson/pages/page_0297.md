Consider again the case for a minimum glide angle as treated by Eq. (5.126). For a typical modern airplane, $(L/D)_{\max} = 15$, and for this case from Eq. (5.126), $\theta_{\min} = 3.8^\circ$—a small angle. Hence, we can reasonably assume $\cos \theta = 1$ for such cases. Recall from Eq. (5.30) that

$$
\left(\frac{L}{D}\right)_{\max} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{4C_{D,0}K}}
$$

[5.30]

and for $L = W$ (consistent with the assumption of $\cos \theta = 1$), the velocity at which $L/D$ is maximum is given by Eq. (5.34)

$$
V_{(L/D)_{\max}} = \left(\frac{2}{\rho_\infty}\sqrt{\frac{K}{C_{D,0}}}\frac{W}{S}\right)^{1/2}
$$

[5.34]

Hence, for $\theta_{\min}$, the equilibrium velocity along the glide path is given by Eq. (5.34).

## Example 5.14

Consider the Gulfstream IV flying at 30,000 ft. Assume a total loss of engine thrust. Calculate (a) the minimum glide path angle, (b) the maximum range covered over the ground, and (c) the corresponding equilibrium glide velocity at 30,000 ft and at sea level.

### Solution

(a) From Eq. (5.126)

$$
\tan \theta_{\min} = \frac{1}{(L/D)_{\max}}
$$

From Example (5.4), $(L/D)_{\max} = 14.43$. Hence

$$
\tan \theta_{\min} = \frac{1}{14.43} = 0.0693
$$

$$
\theta_{\min} = 3.964^\circ
$$

(b) From Fig. 5.40,

$$
\frac{h}{R} = \tan \theta
$$

Hence,

$$
R_{\max} = \frac{h}{\tan \theta_{\min}} = \frac{30{,}000}{\tan \theta_{\min}} = \frac{30{,}000}{0.0693} = 432{,}900\ \text{ft} \cong 82\ \text{mi}
$$

(c) At 30,000 ft, $\rho_\infty = 8.9068 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{slug/ft}^3$. From Eq. (5.34),

$$
V_{(L/D)_{\max}} = \left(\frac{2}{\rho_\infty}\sqrt{\frac{K}{C_{D,0}}}\frac{W}{S}\right)^{1/2}
= \left[\frac{2}{8.9068 \times 10^{-4}}\sqrt{\frac{0.08}{0.015}}(76.84)\right]^{1/2}
= 631.2\ \text{ft/s}
$$
