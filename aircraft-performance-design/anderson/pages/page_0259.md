Above this velocity, the drag is assumed to follow a sharp, linear increase with a slope given by the normalized value obtained from Fig. 5.21. Since Fig. 5.22 shows drag versus velocity, this normalized slope in terms of $V_\infty$ is obtained as follows. Since, $M = V/a$, then

$$
dM=\frac{dV}{a}
$$

Thus, the normalized slope in terms of $V_\infty$ is

$$
\frac{d[D/D_1]}{dV_\infty}=\frac{d[D/D_1]}{dM_\infty}\frac{dM_\infty}{dV_\infty}
=\frac{d[D/D_1]}{dM_\infty}\frac{1}{a_\infty}
=\frac{14.3}{994.7}
=0.01438\ (\text{ft/s})^{-1}
$$

The actual slope as shown in Fig. 5.22 is not normalized and is given by

$$
\frac{dD}{dV_\infty}=D_1\frac{d[D/D_1]}{dV_\infty}=0.01438D_1
$$

The drag-divergence point shown in Fig. 5.22 is at $V_{DD}=815.7$ ft/s, which corresponds to a drag $D_{DD}=5{,}750$ lb. Let $D_1=D_{DD}$. Then

$$
\frac{dD}{dV_\infty}=0.01438D_{DD}=0.01438(5{,}750)=82.7\ \text{lb(ft/s)}^{-1}
