## TABLE 8.12 Position of flexural center $Q$ for different sections

| Form of section | Position of $Q$ |
|---|---|
| 1. Any narrow section symmetrical about the $x$ axis; centroid at $x = 0$, $y = 0$  ![Figure 8.12-1: narrow symmetrical section](figures/fig8_12_1.png) <!-- Description: A narrow section symmetric about the x-axis with coordinates x and y shown, width a, elemental strip dx, thickness t, and flexural center offset e. --> | $e=\dfrac{1+3\nu}{1+\nu}\dfrac{\int xt^3dx}{\int t^3dx}$  For narrow triangle (with $\nu = 0.25$), $e = 0.187a$ (Refs. 32 and 52) |
| 2. Beam composed of $n$ elements of any form, connected of separate, with common neutral axis (e.g., multiple-spar airplane wing)  ![Figure 8.12-2: beam composed of n elements](figures/fig8_12_2.png) <!-- Description: Multiple separate beam elements labeled 1, 2, 3, ..., n with distances x2, x3, xn from a reference, and flexural center Q offset by e. --> | $e=\dfrac{E_2I_2x_2+E_3I_3x_3+\cdots+E_nI_nx_n}{E_1I_1+E_2I_2+E_3I_3+\cdots+E_nI_n}$  where $I_1$, $I_2$, etc., are moments of inertia of the several elements about the $X$ axis (that is, $Q$ is at the centroid of the products $EI$ for the several elements) |
| 3. Semicircular area  ![Figure 8.12-3: semicircular area](figures/fig8_12_3.png) <!-- Description: A semicircular area of radius R with centroid O and flexural center Q shown to the right of the centroid by distance e. --> | $e=\dfrac{8}{15\pi}\dfrac{3+4\nu}{1+\nu}R$ ($Q$ is to right of centroid) (Refs. 1 and 64)  For any sector of solid or hollow circular area, see Ref. 32 |
| 4. Angle  ![Figure 8.12-4: angle section](figures/fig8_12_4.png) <!-- Description: An L-angle section with legs of widths w1 and w2 and heights h1 and h2, central axes X1, X2, Y1, Y2, centroid O, and flexural center Q with offsets ex and ey. --> | Leg 1 = rectangle $w_1h_1$; leg 2 = rectangle $w_2h_2$  $I_1$ = moment of inertia of leg 1 about $Y_1$ (central axis)  $I_2$ = moment of inertia of leg about $Y_2$ (central axis)  $e_y=\dfrac{h_1}{2}\dfrac{I_1}{I_1+I_2}$ (for $e_x$ use $X_1$ and $X_2$ central axes) (Ref. 31)  If $w_1$ and $w_2$ are small, $e_x=e_y=0$ (practically) and $Q$ is at $0$ |
| 5. Channel  ![Figure 8.12-5: channel section](figures/fig8_12_5.png) <!-- Description: A channel section with axes X and Y through centroid O, dimensions b, h, t, and flexural center Q offset by e from the web centerline. --> | $e=h\dfrac{I_{xy}}{I_x}$  where $I_{xy}$ = product of inertia of the half section (above $X$) with respect to axes $X$ and $Y$, and $I_x$ = moment of inertia of whole section with respect to axis $X$  If $t$ is uniform, $e=(b^2-t^2/4)h^2t/4I_x$ |
| 6. T  ![Figure 8.12-6: T section](figures/fig8_12_6.png) <!-- Description: A T-section with flange thickness t2, web thickness t1, depths d1 and d2, centroid O, flexural center Q, and offset e. --> | $e=\dfrac{1}{2}(t_1+t_2)\dfrac{1}{1+d_1^3t_1/d_2^3t_2}$  For a T-beam of ordinary proportions, $Q$ may be assumed to be at $0$ |
