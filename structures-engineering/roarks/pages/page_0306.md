Consider the equivalent loading shown in Fig. 9.13(b), where $R_B = -333.3 \,\text{lb}$ and $R_A = -1000 \,\text{lb}$. The only difference is in the point of zero deflection. Owing to the symmetry of loading, one-half of the ring can be considered slope-guided at both ends, points $A$ and $B$. Case 1f gives tabulated values of the necessary coefficients for $\phi = 180^\circ$ and $\theta = 60^\circ$. We can now solve for the following values:

$$
V_A = -666.7(0.75) = -500 \,\text{lb}
$$

$$
M_A = -666.7(100)(-0.5774) = 38,490 \,\text{lb-in}
$$

$$
\psi_A = \frac{-666.7(100^2)}{EI}(-0.2722) = \frac{1.815(10^6)}{EI}
$$

$$
T_A = 0 \qquad y_A = 0 \qquad \Theta_A = 0
$$

$$
M_B = -666.7(100)(-0.2887) = 19,250 \,\text{lb-in}
$$

$$
M_{60^\circ} = -666.7(100)(0.3608) = -24,050 \,\text{lb-in}
$$

The equations for $M$ and $T$ can now be examined to determine the location of the maximum combined stress:

$$
M_x = -50{,}000 \sin x + 38{,}490 \cos x + 66{,}667 \sin (x - 60^\circ)\langle x - 60^\circ \rangle^0
$$

$$
T_x = -50{,}000(1 - \cos x) + 38{,}490 \sin x + 66{,}667[1 - \cos (x - 60^\circ)]\langle x - 60^\circ \rangle^0
$$

A careful examination of the expression for $M$ shows no maximum values except at the ends and at the position of the load. The torque, however, has a maximum value of 13,100 in-lb at $x = 37.59^\circ$ and a minimum value of $-8790$ in-lb at $x = 130.9^\circ$. At these same locations the bending moments are zero. At the position of the load, the torque $T = 8330$ lb-in. Nowhere is the combined stress larger than the bending stress at point $A$. Therefore,

$$
\sigma_A = 20{,}000 = \frac{M_A c}{I} = \frac{38{,}490d/2}{(\pi/64)d^4} = \frac{392{,}000}{d^3}
$$

which gives

$$
d = 2.70 \,\text{in} \qquad \text{and} \qquad I = 2.609 \,\text{in}^4
$$
