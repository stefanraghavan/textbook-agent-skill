| 15. Thin-walled circular tube under uniform longitudinal compression (radius of tube = $r$)

![Figure 15: Thin-walled circular tube under uniform longitudinal compression](figures/fig15.png)
<!-- Description: A thin-walled cylindrical tube under uniform axial compressive stress $\sigma$ applied at both ends. -->

$\dfrac{r}{t} > 10$ | 15a. Ends not constrained | $\sigma' = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}\sqrt{1-\nu^2}} \dfrac{E}{r} t$ $(Refs. 6, 12, 13, 24)$

Most accurate for very long tubes, but applicable if length is several times as great as $1.72\sqrt{rt}$, which is the length of a half-wave of buckling. Tests indicate an actual buckling strength of 40–60% of this theoretical value, or $\sigma' = 0.3Et/r$ approximately |
| 16. Thin-walled circular tube under a transverse bending moment $M$ (radius of tube = $r$)

![Figure 16: Thin-walled circular tube under a transverse bending moment](figures/fig16.png)
<!-- Description: A thin-walled cylindrical tube subjected to equal and opposite bending moments $M$ at its ends. -->

$\dfrac{r}{t} > 10$ | 16a. No constraint | $M' = K\frac{E}{1-\nu^2}rt^2$

Here the theoretical value of $K$ for pure bending and long tubes is 0.99. The average value of $K$ determined by tests is 1.14, and the minimum value is 0.72. Except for very short tubes, length effect is negligible and a small transverse shear produces no appreciable reduction in $M'$. A very short cylinder under transverse (beam) shear may fail by buckling at neutral axis when shear stress there reaches a value of about $1.25\tau'$ for case 17a $(Refs. 6, 14, 15)$ |
| 17. Thin-walled circular tube under a twisting moment $T$ that produces a uniform circumferential shear stress:

$\tau = \dfrac{T}{2\pi r^2 t}$

(length of tube = $l$; radius of tube = $r$)

![Figure 17: Thin-walled circular tube under a twisting moment](figures/fig17.png)
<!-- Description: A thin-walled cylindrical tube subjected to twisting moments $T$ at both ends, producing circumferential shear stress. -->

$\dfrac{r}{t} > 10$ | 17a. Ends hinged, i.e., wall free to change angle with cross section, but circular section maintained | $\tau' = \dfrac{E}{1-\nu^2}\left(\dfrac{t}{l}\right)^2 \left(1.27 + \sqrt{9.64 + 0.466H^{1.5}}\right)$

where $H = \sqrt{1-\nu^2}\dfrac{l^2}{tr}$

Tests indicate that the actual buckling stress is 60–75% of this theoretical value, with the majority of the data points nearer 75% $(Refs. 6, 16, 18, 25)$ |
|  | 17b. Ends clamped, i.e., wall held perpendicular to cross section and circular section maintained | $\tau' \dfrac{E}{1-\nu^2}\left(\dfrac{t}{l}\right)^2 \left(-2.39 + \sqrt{96.9 + 0.605H^{1.5}}\right)$

where $H$ is given in part 17a.

The statement in part a regarding actual buckling stress applies here as well $(Refs. 6, 16, 18, 25)$ |
