## 11.4 Bimetallic Plates

A very wide beam of rectangular cross section can be treated as a beam if $E$ is replaced by $E/(1-\nu^2)$ and $I$ by $t^3/12$ (see Sec. 8.11). It can also be treated as a plate with two opposite edges free as shown in Figs. 8.16 and 11.2. For details see Ref. 88.

To use the beam equations in Tables 8.1, 8.5, 8.6, 8.8, and 8.9 for plates like that shown in Fig. 11.2 with two opposite edges free, the loadings must be uniformly distributed across the plate parallel to side $b$ as shown. At every position in such a plate, except close to the free edges $a$, there will be bending moments $M_z = \nu M_x$. If the plate is isotropic and homogeneous, and in the absence of any in-plane loading, there will be no change in length of any line parallel to side $b$. The response of a *bimetallic plate* differs from that of the homogeneous plate in one important respect. If the values of Poisson's ratio differ for the two materials, there will be a change in length of those lines parallel to side $b$ due to an in-plane strain $\epsilon_z$ developed from the loadings shown in Fig. 11.2. Using the notations from Figs. 11.2 and 11.3 and from the expression for $K_{2p}$ in the next paragraph,

$$
\epsilon_z = \frac{6M_x(1-\nu_a^2)}{E_b t_b^2 K_{2p}} \frac{(t_b/t_a)(1+t_b/t_a)(\nu_a-\nu_b)}{(1+E_a t_a/E_b t_b)^2-(\nu_a+\nu_b E_a t_a/E_b t_b)^2}
$$

For the moment loading $M_o$ in Fig. 11.2(a), the value of $\epsilon_z$ will be everywhere the same and the plate will merely expand or contract in the $z$ direction. For the line loading shown in Fig. 11.2(c), however, the unit strains $\epsilon_z$ will differ from place to place depending upon the value of $M_x$, and consequently in-plane stresses will be developed. For more general analyses of this type of problem see Refs. 89 and 90.
